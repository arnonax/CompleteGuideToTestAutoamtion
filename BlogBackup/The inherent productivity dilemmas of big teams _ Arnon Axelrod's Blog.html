The inherent productivity dilemmas of big teams

February 17, 2013

facebook linkedin twitter email
one comment

Most people say that they’re more productive when they work alone, in private, without anything or anyone to disturb them. This claim is even backed up by various researches that show, among other things, that working in a noisy environment detract from the employees’ ability to solve complex problems.

Microsoft took the conclusion of that research and implemented it in its own premises. At Microsoft almost each employee has his own room.

All of that is viable and true as long the individual has all the information and all the knowledge he needs in order to complete the task at hand, or at least has immediate access to it (like searching the web, etc.). But what happens when he doesn’t?

Let’s say that John is a developer in a Scrum team. At the Planning meeting John committed to a particular task that he should deliver during the sprint. At first he believes he has all the information he needs in order to complete his task, but half way through the sprint he notices that his code must interact with another component that he doesn’t know very deeply. Note that in this case John knows that he doesn’t know something. This is often referred to as 1st Order Ignorance.

In this case, John has to ask his team member Brad, who wrote the mentioned component, for some help. If Brad is free and available right now – there’s no problem. John gets the answer and continues to work seamlessly. But apparently Brad is currently very busy solving a hard problem that is related to another task, so John has to either wait for Brad to become available, or guess the answer and continue with the direction he has chosen, with the risk of having to undo everything he done until Brad became available and gave him the right answer.

So we may conclude that for a team of developers to be most productive, everyone in the team should be as responsive and as available as possible in order not to block other team members.

But here lies the catch 22: If everyone has to be as responsive and as available as possible, that means that John may be interrupted from time to time in order to help others, distracting him from his own task. As long as the team is pretty small (most Scrum teams I known are no more than 10 people), this is probably not a big deal. But when the project becomes bigger and bigger, and there are many Scrum teams that need to interact with each other, the frequency of the interruptions increases and it can become a problem.

The worst part of our story comes when John demonstrated the new feature he developed during the sprint review meeting, and there were some major gaps between what the Product Owner expected and what John has developed. When they discussed these gaps they understand that John wasn’t aware of these aspects whatsoever, and now when he is, he understands that he had to design his solution differently. Also they found that 3 out of 8 members in the team weren’t aware of this issue. Fortunately enough, because our team works in Scrum, the gap was found only after 1 sprint (and not toward the end of the project), but still it’s far from being effective. Note that in this case John didn’t know that he didn’t know something – which is often referred to as 2nd Order Ignorance.

If only John and any of the other 5 developers that were aware of that issue were talking about John’s solution earlier, this incident could be prevented much earlier. But also in this case – when the team becomes bigger – it’s not feasible to have everyone talk to everyone else all the time. No matter how efficiently the organization uses electronic communication means (emails, phone, wiki’s, IM, etc.), the larger the organization that has to co-operate and to share knowledge is, there will be more knowledge that someone has and someone else needs, but even though they won’t know about one another. (i.e. 2nd Order Ignorance). In fact, the amount of communication between n people is at order of n2.

Probably the main reason for this is that communication takes time. Whether it’s to read emails, read written documentation (in whatever form), or having a conversation (whether through phone, IM or fact-to-fact) – it takes time.

Note also that the larger the team is, the chance of 2nd order ignorance is increased dramatically, and is harder to mitigate.

Another related dilemma is the check-in rate. In general it is advised (especially in an Agile context) to work in small chunks and check-in often. This way everyone get feedback quicker sooner.

Using a Continuous Integration system, in order to maintain a “green” build, every developer must build and run all the necessary tests locally before check-in. While this takes shouldn’t take too long, if the team is big and the check-in rate is high – there’s a good chance that between the moment that John started the local build and the moment it’s completed – someone else checked-in new code. This means that the code the John is about to check-in is no longer the latest and potentially the build will break. Again – the larger the team is, the chances this happens is higher. Also – chances are that the larger the team and the complex the system is, the longer is the build and the unit-tests to run.

Using Gated Check-in can minimize the chances of such incidents, but not eliminate it completely. Note that in order to use Gated Check-in in a large project – the throughput of the build system must be in average at least as the rate of the check-ins. For example, given one build agent, if in average someone checks-in changes every 15 minutes, and a build cycle takes 20 minutes – then the system will bloat. On the other hand, if there are 2 build agents, the throughput becomes a build every 10 minutes (which is less than 15) and everything’s fine. Given our previous assumption that bigger teams and more complex projects means longer build/test times and more frequent check-ins, means that we need more build agents. More build agents by itself is a solvable problem, but it also introduces another problem: while one build agent builds and tests John’s changes, another build agent may be building and testing Brad’s changes separately. Even if each of these changes are passing all the tests separately, there’s a chance that when they’ll be committed and merged into the trunk they will break on another (and the worst thing is that now you won’t know about it until the next check-in, because the build and the tests were already passing on these changes!)

Note that I found these problems very similar to the CAP theorem problem of scalability, even though I didn’t find a clear correlation between the two. Maybe you will?

To conclude – unfortunately I don’t have a silver bullet answer for these problems. I guess that every big team must find the point of less friction to make it most effective. Clearly you can chunk the big team to smaller teams – but that won’t necessarily help because this way you’re in effect preventing communication flow in an arbitrary and artificial manner. So my only suggestion to you would be to be aware of these dilemmas and make your own tradeoffs…

As always – I’ll be glad to hear your thoughts about these issues!
